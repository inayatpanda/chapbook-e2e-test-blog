There is a particular kind of person who will not write in a book. You know them. They keep the receipt as a bookmark, crack the spine no further than ninety degrees, and recoil at a folded corner as though you had proposed setting the thing on fire. To them a book is a museum piece: to be admired, preserved, and on no account touched with a pencil. I want, gently, to talk them out of it.

A book you have written in is not a damaged book. It is a book you have actually read.

## The margins were always meant to be used

The instinct that the page is sacred is surprisingly recent. For most of the history of the written word, the margin was where the real action happened. Medieval scribes copying manuscripts left wide, deliberate borders and then filled them — with translations, corrections, cross-references, arguments with the author, and, when the work got dull, doodles of rabbits doing unspeakable things to knights. The word itself gives the game away: *marginalia* simply means "things in the margin", and we have it because there was so much of it to need a name.

These weren't defacements. They were the conversation. A serious book passed from reader to reader accumulating a second text in its borders, so that owning a well-annotated copy meant inheriting every clever mind that had held it before you. The blank, pristine margin — the one we now treat as inviolable — would have struck a medieval scholar as a sign that nobody had bothered to think.

## Some of the best thinking lives in the margins

The history of the marginal note is, quietly, a history of ideas. The most famous of all is Fermat's: a single line scrawled beside a problem, claiming a proof he didn't have room to write down, which then tormented mathematicians for three and a half centuries.[^fermat] An entire field of modern mathematics grew, in part, out of a man jotting in a margin and then dying before anyone could ask him to elaborate.

Others wrote in their books so well that the notes became literature in their own right. Coleridge annotated so compulsively, and so wittily, that people queued up to lend him their books in the hope of getting them back improved.[^coleridge] His scribbles are now studied as carefully as his poems. The point is not that you, too, will produce publishable genius in your margins — you almost certainly won't, and neither will I — but that the practice of arguing back at a book in writing is exactly what reading attentively looks like. The note is the residue of thought.

## In pencil, at least

I will grant the sceptic one concession, and it is the whole of my case: do it in pencil. Pencil erases. Pencil is humble. Pencil admits that today's blinding insight is tomorrow's slightly embarrassing underline, and lets you take it back. With a pencil in hand you stop reading like a tourist photographing a cathedral and start reading like someone with a stake in the argument — underlining the line that lands, flagging the bit that doesn't, writing *no* in the margin and meaning it.

The book improves, too. A re-read becomes a conversation with your former self: here is what struck you at twenty, here is where you were plainly wrong, here is the joke you missed the first time. A clean copy remembers nothing. An annotated one remembers everything.

So write in your books. Underline, argue, doodle the rabbits if you must. The pristine first edition gathering dust on the shelf is not being respected — it is being ignored. A book is not a relic to be guarded. It is a conversation, and it is impolite to say nothing back.
