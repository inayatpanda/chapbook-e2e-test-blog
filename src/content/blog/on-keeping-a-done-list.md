The to-do list is the only document you keep that is designed never to be finished. You add three things, you cross off one, and somewhere in the same afternoon two more arrive to take its place. It is less a plan than a tide. Whatever you do, the level stays roughly the same, and you go to bed each night staring at a list that is still, stubbornly, full.

This is not a failure of discipline. It is the basic arithmetic of being alive and having responsibilities. But the format quietly lies to you about it. A to-do list only ever shows you the undone — the backlog, the debt, the not-yet. It is a ledger that records only what you owe and never what you've paid. Spend long enough with it and you can put in a genuinely good day's work and still feel, at the close of it, vaguely behind.

## The list that only counts forwards

I started keeping a second list almost by accident. At the end of the day, instead of looking at what was left, I wrote down what I'd actually done — not the grand items, the real ones. Replied to the thing I'd been avoiding for a week. Fixed the bug. Read the chapter. Had the awkward conversation. Cooked something that wasn't toast.

The first surprise was how much there was. A day that had felt like wading through treacle turned out, written down, to contain eleven finished things. The to-do list had shown me the four I hadn't reached. The done list showed me the eleven I had. Same day. Wildly different verdict, depending purely on which column you let yourself read.

The second surprise was that the done list does not lie in the convenient direction. You can't pad it. A to-do list is full of aspiration — "learn Italian", "sort out the loft" — items that can sit there for years looking like intentions. A done list only admits things that genuinely happened. It is, of all the lists you keep, the one most allergic to nonsense.

## Why this actually works

There's a real finding underneath the smugness. The researchers Teresa Amabile and Steven Kramer spent years collecting daily diary entries from people doing creative work — thousands of them — to find out what actually moves the needle on motivation from one day to the next. Not pay, not pressure, not the occasional grand triumph. The single biggest factor was what they called the progress principle: the sense of making meaningful headway, even small headway, in work you care about.

The catch is that progress is the part we are worst at noticing. The brain is a magnificent instrument for detecting threats and unfinished business and almost useless at registering the things that went fine. The to-do list flatters that bias — it is a machine for showing you the gap. The done list does the unglamorous opposite. It makes the progress visible, which is precisely the bit that, on the evidence, keeps you going.

## In praise of the boring record

None of this requires an app, a system, or a productivity philosophy with a name and a founder. It requires a line at the bottom of the day and the small honesty to fill it in. Some evenings the list is long and you feel quietly pleased. Some evenings it reads "two emails and survived", and even that is worth knowing, because the alternative was believing you'd done nothing at all.

The to-do list will outlive you; that is its nature, and you should make a sort of peace with it. The done list is the one that tells the truth about the day — that something, however unspectacular, got finished. Keep the first if you must. But write down the second. It is the only one of the two that will ever be on your side.
