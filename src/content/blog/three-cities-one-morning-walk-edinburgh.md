The third and final walk. We end somewhere the morning often arrives with weather attached: Edinburgh.

## Grey, and proud of it

Edinburgh does mornings in grey, and means it as a compliment. A *haar* — the cold sea fog that rolls in off the Forth — can swallow the Old Town whole, leaving spires and chimney pots floating in the murk like a city half-remembered. It ought to be gloomy. It is, instead, completely magnificent, and best met on foot before the cafés open.

The move is to climb. Arthur's Seat — an extinct volcano sitting improbably in the middle of the city — is a twenty-minute scramble that lays the whole place at your feet: the castle on its rock, the long spine of the Royal Mile, the sea doing something dramatic in the distance. Come back down through the Old Town and the walk turns medieval: *closes* and *wynds*, those narrow stone alleys, peel off the main street into shadow — and, more often than not, into a grisly story.

It is a city that looks written by someone with a flair for atmosphere — which, given how many writers it has produced, is probably fair. After Lisbon's light and Kyoto's hush, Edinburgh offers the third thing a morning can be: a little haunted, a little cold, and absolutely worth getting up for.

Three cities, three mornings, one stubborn little habit. Set the alarm. The hour before a place wakes up is the one it keeps for itself — and, if you're up, for you.
