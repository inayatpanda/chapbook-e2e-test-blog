The second walk. If Lisbon wakes slowly, Kyoto is already awake and would rather you kept your voice down.

## The case for the early alarm

There is a particular reason to rise early in Kyoto, and it has nothing to do with the weather. It's the buses. The famous temples — the golden one, the thousand vermilion gates — are sublime at seven and a scrum by ten, and the entire difference between the two is a matter of when you set your alarm. A morning walk here buys you the one thing the brochures can't sell: emptiness.

The Philosopher's Path is the obvious choice, and for once the obvious choice is right. A narrow canal, cherry trees leaning over it, a stone walk that a real philosopher really did pace while thinking. At dawn it is just water, the occasional jogger, and the sound of a city politely declining to shout. The side streets hand you raked gravel gardens through half-open gates, wooden *machiya* houses, and a cat conducting some important and private business.

Kyoto rewards restraint — in the architecture, in the gardens, and in the visitor. Walk slowly, say little, and the city tells you more. It is the precise opposite of Lisbon's cheerful sprawl, which is exactly the point of doing this in three cities: the same hour, spent in three completely different ways.

Last stop: somewhere colder, greyer, and rather more haunted — Edinburgh.
