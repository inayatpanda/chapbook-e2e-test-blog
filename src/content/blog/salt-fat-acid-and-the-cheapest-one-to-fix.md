Something is wrong with the soup. You've followed the recipe, you've used decent ingredients, you've simmered it for the requisite hour, and it tastes like a photograph of soup. Flat. Polite. Missing something. So you do what most of us do at this point, which is rummage. A spoon of pesto. A glug of cream. A heroic grating of parmesan over a thing that did not ask for parmesan. You are throwing money at a problem that costs almost nothing to fix, and you are throwing it in the wrong direction.

The chef Samin Nosrat built a whole book — and a rather good Netflix series — around the idea that good cooking comes down to four things: salt, fat, acid, heat. It's an elegant way to think, because it sorts the chaos of cooking into levers you can actually pull. And the genuinely useful part, the bit worth pinning above the hob, is that two of those four levers are dirt cheap and do most of the heavy lifting. The other two get all the attention and most of the budget.

## The one you're underusing

Nine times out of ten, "missing something" means salt. Not because salt makes food *taste salty* — that's the failure mode, not the goal — but because it makes food taste more like itself. It suppresses bitterness, sharpens sweetness, and pulls flavours up out of the background where they've been sulking. The same tomato sauce, unsalted, tastes thin and slightly sour; properly salted, it tastes like tomatoes turned up to where they were always meant to be.

The trick almost nobody is taught is *when*. Most home cooks salt once, at the very end, panic slightly, and stop. But salt added early has time to work its way into things — into the onions as they soften, into the water the pasta actually cooks in, into the meat before it hits the pan. Season in layers, taste as you go, and you arrive somewhere seasoned all the way through rather than seasoned on top. A pot you've salted in three small instalments over an hour will always beat the same pot hit with one frightened pinch at the death.

And it costs nothing. A kilogram of salt is the price of a single fancy garnish, and it will outlast several of your better intentions.

## The second-cheapest miracle

Say you've salted properly and the dish is still not *singing*. It's rich, it's savoury, but it sits heavily, like a guest who won't take the hint and leave. That's almost always a job for acid: a squeeze of lemon, a splash of vinegar, a spoonful of the brine from the jar of whatever.

Acid is the brightness lever. It cuts through fat, lifts a stew out of its own gravy, and stops a rich thing from feeling like a lie-down. Restaurant food often tastes better than yours for exactly this reason — not some secret stock simmered for a fortnight, but a final hit of lemon or sherry vinegar you weren't there to see. A lentil soup that tastes muddy and worthy will, with one teaspoon of red wine vinegar, suddenly taste like someone competent made it on purpose.

Like salt, acid rewards going slowly. Add a little, taste, add a little more. You are listening for the moment the whole thing leans forward and pays attention. Overshoot and you've made it sour; creep up on it and you've made it alive.

## Why we reach for the expensive thing instead

So why do we keep buying truffle oil and aged balsamic and tiny ruinous jars of saffron when the fix is in the cupboard for pennies? Partly because salt and acid feel too simple to be the answer — surely a flat dish needs *more*, not a pinch of the cheapest thing in the kitchen. Partly because the food industry would much rather sell you a £9 finishing sauce than remind you that you already own the two ingredients that would have done it.

But mostly, I think, because tasting as you go is a habit, and habits take attention, and attention is the one ingredient that genuinely isn't cheap. Salt the onions, taste. Splash the vinegar, taste. It is almost insultingly low-tech.

So before you reach for the cream, reach for the salt cellar and the lemon. Your soup wasn't missing an ingredient. It was missing a cook who kept tasting it — and that one's free.
