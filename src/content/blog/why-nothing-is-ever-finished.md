The French poet Paul Valéry left us one of the most useful sentences anyone has written about making things: a poem is never finished, only abandoned. He meant it about verse, which he revised obsessively, but he had accidentally described almost everything. Software is abandoned. Paintings are abandoned. Essays, side projects, kitchen renovations, novels, this very sentence — abandoned, every one. We just dress it up in nicer language and call it "done".

That sounds bleak for about four seconds, and then it becomes oddly freeing. If nothing is ever truly finished, then "finished" was never a property of the work in the first place. It is a decision. Someone looks at the thing and says: that'll do. The clock, the budget, or sheer exhaustion makes the call, and we backfill a story about completeness afterwards.

## The state that never arrives

We talk about finishing as though it were a destination — a line painted on the road the work eventually rolls across. It isn't. There is always one more pass. The paragraph could be tighter, the colour warmer, the function ready for one more edge case nobody will ever hit. Da Vinci reportedly fussed over the Mona Lisa for years and carried it around, still tinkering, until he died. He did not finish it. He stopped, because he ran out of life, which is the most honest deadline there is.

Look closely at anything you admire and you will find the seams where someone decided to stop. The novel that trails off in the final third. The building with the awkward bit round the back where the money ran out. The album with one track that is clearly the sound of a band wanting to go home. None of it is a flaw, exactly. It is the fingerprint of a human being choosing an end.

## The punishing last twenty per cent

Here is the cruel mathematics of making things. The first eighty per cent goes quickly and feels wonderful: you sketch, you draft, you build, and progress is visible by the hour. Then you hit the last twenty per cent, and time stops behaving. The polish, the corner cases, the thousand small wrongnesses you can feel but not name — that final stretch can swallow as much effort as everything before it combined. Programmers have a rueful joke about this: the first ninety per cent of the work takes ninety per cent of the time, and the remaining ten per cent takes the other ninety per cent.

This is where perfectionism quietly does its damage. It dresses up as high standards, which sound admirable, but its real effect is to keep you forever sanding a surface no one else will ever touch. The perfectionist is not chasing quality. They are avoiding the moment of judgement — the exposing act of declaring something good enough and letting other people see it.

## Knowing when to stop is the actual skill

We celebrate the wrong talent. We praise starting — the bold idea, the blank page bravely faced — and we say almost nothing about the unglamorous craft of stopping well. Yet that is the rarer gift. Anyone can keep fiddling. Knowing the precise moment when another hour will make the thing worse, not better — when you have started adding noise instead of signal — is hard-won.

Shipping is not the opposite of caring. It is what caring looks like once it grows up. The work that stays in the drawer, endlessly almost-ready, pleases no one, least of all you. A thing released into the world, slightly imperfect, can be used, argued with, built upon. A thing kept safe from judgement is just a private museum of your own anxiety.

So no, this post is not finished. I could hunt down the exact wording of Valéry's line, sharpen the joke in the third section, cut a paragraph that is only just pulling its weight. I could keep going for another week. Instead, in the proper spirit of the thing, I am going to do what every maker eventually does to everything they have made. I am going to abandon it. There — done.
