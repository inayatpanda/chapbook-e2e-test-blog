For almost all of human history, purple was the colour of the obscenely rich. The good stuff — Tyrian purple — was wrung from a Mediterranean sea snail, and it took something like ten thousand of them to dye a single gram of cloth. It cost more than gold. Roman emperors hoarded it; to be *born to the purple* was not a metaphor but a wardrobe. For three thousand years, if you saw that colour, you were looking at money.

Then a teenager ruined the whole racket by accident.

Over the Easter holiday of 1856, an eighteen-year-old chemistry student named William Perkin was trying, in a makeshift lab at the top of his parents' house, to synthesise quinine — the only treatment for malaria, and desperately scarce. He failed completely. What he got was a sludgy black mess. But as he cleaned the flask out with alcohol, the gunk dissolved into a startling, brilliant purple. He had been chasing a medicine and stumbled into **mauveine**: the first synthetic dye.

What happened next was the Victorian internet losing its mind over a colour. *Mauve mania* swept Britain and France; Queen Victoria wore it to a royal wedding, the Empress Eugénie decided it matched her eyes, and *Punch* mocked the whole country for catching "the mauve measles." Perkin — sensibly — had patented the process, opened a factory, and become rich before he was twenty-one.

But the dye was never really the story. Chasing brighter, cheaper colours turned synthetic organic chemistry from a curiosity into an industry — and that industry, especially the German dye giants BASF, Bayer and Hoechst, built the laboratories, the techniques and the fortunes that went on to make modern medicines. The aspirin in your bathroom cabinet is a direct descendant of the hunt for a better purple. An entire branch of science, and a good chunk of the pharmaceutical world, traces back to a schoolboy failing to cure malaria and refusing to throw away the mess.

So the next time you pull on something in a colour that would once have bankrupted a Caesar, that's the inheritance. Colour stopped being a luxury and became chemistry — and it started with one wrong answer that nobody could be bothered to bin.
