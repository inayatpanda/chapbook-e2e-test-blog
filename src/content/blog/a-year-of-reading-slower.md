At some point, reading quietly turned into a sport. Every January, several million people open an app, type a number into a box, and pledge to read that many books before December. The app keeps score. It draws a little progress bar. It tells you, helpfully, that you are "4 books behind schedule," as though a novel were a train you have managed to miss.

I did this for years. I hit my number, posted the satisfying year-end grid of covers, and could not, three weeks later, tell you what most of them were about. I had read forty-something books and retained roughly one and a half. The scoreboard was full. I was empty. It took me an embarrassingly long time to notice that nobody keeps a tally of how many meals they have eaten, or how many conversations, and that the things which actually feed you tend to resist being counted.

## Reading to finish is not reading to keep

There are two completely different activities wearing the same word. One is reading to *finish*: eyes moving over every line in order until the last page, at which point the book is Done and may be logged. The other is reading to *keep*: slowing where it earns slowing, stopping to argue in the margin, putting the thing down for a week because a single paragraph has rearranged something and you need to walk around with it.

The scoreboard only rewards the first kind. Worse, it actively punishes the second, because re-reading scores zero. A book you have read before adds nothing to the total, which is a strange way to treat the highest compliment you can pay a writer. The books that changed me, I have read more than once — and not one of those re-reads counted toward a single annual challenge. By the metric, the most valuable reading I do is worthless.

## The marginalia test

Here is the honest measure I have started using, and it has nothing to do with numbers. A month after finishing something, can I argue with it? Not summarise it — anyone can summarise — but actually take a position, agree with this bit, push back on that one, remember where it changed my mind. If I can, the book is genuinely mine now. If I can't, I didn't really read it; I commuted through it.

What gets a book over that line, almost every time, is annotation. Writing in the margins feels like vandalism the first few times — a hangover from school, where the books belonged to someone else. But a marked-up book is a record of you thinking, and thinking is the entire point. The underlines are where you woke up. Mortimer Adler put it about as plainly as it can be put: marking a book is *literally an expression of your differences or your agreements of opinion* with the author. It is reading carried out with your sleeves rolled up.

## It is harder than it sounds

I would love to report that I now drift serenely through six perfect books a year, fully transformed by each. I do not. Slow reading is genuinely difficult, and the difficulty is not the books — it is you, reaching for your phone at the first dull paragraph, mistaking a finished book for an understood one, feeling the old itch to *get through* the stack rather than into a single volume.

The pile by the bed does not shrink any faster for being read carefully; if anything it grows, because a good book sends you off after three more. The scoreboard, at least, offered the clean dopamine of a number going up. Reading to keep offers something quieter and far more inconvenient: the slow, lopsided business of being changed by what you read, on no schedule, with nothing to post at the end of it. Which is, I have decided, the only version worth doing — even if it means finishing the year four books behind, and richer for it.
